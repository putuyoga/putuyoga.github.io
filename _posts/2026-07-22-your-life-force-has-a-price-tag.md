Today I want to share something different: my current philosophy about money.

To me, money is a fraction of my time and energy. It only becomes precious once you really sit with that idea. Not because I want to be rich, but because it forces me to confront the value of my own time and energy.

Every penny you earn is, in some way, a piece of your life exchanged for it. The hours you worked. The stress you endured. The decisions you made. The energy you spent.

## Be Honest

So it's worth asking yourself: what does money actually mean to you? For some people, it's the down payment on a dream house. For others, it's the freedom to enjoy their favorite food without thinking twice.

There's nothing wrong with either. Everyone's needs are different. But it pays to be mindful of how you spend your life force. This isn't about denying yourself things that bring you joy, it's about being aware of the trade-offs.

**Ask yourself:**

> When you buy that expensive gadget every year, are you okay spending your life force on it?
> When you buy that luxury bag just to fit in, are you okay spending your life force on it?
> When you buy that overpriced coffee everyday, are you okay spending your life force on it?
> When you buy that unnecessary subscription just to keep up with trends, are you okay spending your life force on it?

## Buying Back Your Time

There is more to it than that. The more money you have, the more time and energy you can buy back. You can buy convenience. You can buy security. Eventually, you can buy freedom.

How? By delaying gratification, postponing unnecessary wants, and not just saving but investing strategically.

Before we even talk about investing, though, people often forget the basics: save first, build an emergency fund. When you lose your source of income and still have a cushion beneath you, you can think clearly instead of panicking into bad decisions.

## Conclusion

At the end of the day, none of this is about restricting yourself or chasing a bigger number. It's about spending on purpose. Asking yourself the honest question before the purchase, not after. Saving enough that fear doesn't get to make your decisions for you. And investing what's left with intention, so your money starts buying back the very thing you traded for it in the first place — your time.

Money will always ask something of you: your hours, your stress, your energy. The least you can do is ask something back — clarity, on what it's actually for.

**The real currency was never money. It was always time and energy.**
